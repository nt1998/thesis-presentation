Electroencephalography (EEG)

32-channel 10–10 electrode layout
"EEG Recording Cap" by Chris Hope · CC BY 2.0
so what is eeg?
eeg measures the electrical activity of the brain, right at the scalp
variety of devices, from small portable ones with just a few sensors, to expensive or even implanted ones with 32 or more sensors

so its used a lot: epilepsy detection, sleep studies, brain-computer interfaces, and cognitive research
but the raw signal is basically unusable on its own

EEG Artefacts

the reason: various artefacts from non-brain signals
they can be even bigger than the brain activity itself (here you see eye blinks taking over the frontal channels)

they also overlap the brain signal in time and frequency
so you cant just filter or threshold all of them away

Artefact Types

Technical

artefacts can be split in 2 groups:

physiological, so from the body:
ocular: eye movements and blinks, large and slow, mostly frontal
cardiac: the heartbeat, rhythmic spikes
muscle: from muscle contractions, spans all frequencies, hits every sensor - the hardest one

technical, so external:
mainly line noise at 50 hz, plus electrode, movement and environmental noise

line noise we remove in preprocessing
the body ones overlap the brain and have to be separated - thats ica

Independent Component Analysis

X = A·S   →   Ŝ = W·X
ica separates the mixed signal into independent sources

think of the cocktail party problem: you are in a room where many people talk at once, and what you hear is all their voices mixed together
ica is what pulls that mix back apart into the individual voices

here its the same: the electrodes pick up brain activity, blinks and muscle all mixed together, and ica separates them into independent components. we then remove the "bad" ones and use back projection to rebuild the clean noice free version

Manual Component Classification

Figure: MNE-Python, ica.plot_overlay (sample dataset), BSD-3-Clause. Gramfort et al., MEG and EEG data analysis with MNE-Python, Front. Neurosci. 7:267, 2013.
but ica doesnt tell you which component is brain and which is artefact
so an expert goes through every single one and decides: keep or reject

the rejected ones get removed and the clean signal is rebuilt (red before, black after - blinks gone, brain kept)

and this is irreversible: reject a brain component and you lose real signal, keep an artefact and you keep noise
thats why we keep the human in control

and its the bottleneck: alot of participants, ~16 components each

Research Questions

RQ1

Which visualisation and interaction techniques support expert decision-making in ICA artefact classification?

RQ2

How can machine learning recommendations and interactive visualisation techniques be integrated to accelerate classification while preserving expert control?

that leads to two research questions

rq1: which visualization and interaction techniques support the expert in this decision
rq2: how to bring in machine learning to speed it up, while keeping the expert in control

Requirements

The system should support the user in…

  • R1 …receiving visual recommendations on which components to keep or reject.
  • R2 …gaining an overview of all components and supporting exploration, pattern recognition, and context for uncertain classification decisions.
  • R3 …inspecting when and how artefacts appear in the recording over time.
  • R4 …visually distinguishing brain activity from artefacts.
  • R5 …seeing how a component's activity is distributed across the scalp sensors.
  • R6 …logging and exporting classification decisions for reproducibility and team consistency.
from the interviews with the experts we derived six requirements

they fall into three groups: overview and guidance, component inspection, and the workflow itself

the point: every feature in the tool maps back to one of these

Evaluation

  1. Recommendations: review the Tier 1 recommendations, accept three correct ones, and find at least one to overrule
  2. Artefact identification: find a component that could be an eye blink and validate it with the topographic map
  3. Band power analysis: examine a low-scored component's band-power clusters on the timeline and interpret the segments
  4. Mapper exploration: using the Mapper Graph, find a mostly-reject cluster, name its artefact type, and re-check any keep labels
  5. View comparison: compare the Component Cluster and the Mapper Graph and discuss which is more helpful
finally the evaluation

was done with two domain experts, on their own real recordings, thinking aloud
five hands-on tasks that touched every part of the tool

two takeaways:
the order they looked at things matched our layout
and the big one: the cross-participant overview let them catch a component that had been mislabeled

so the value is really the combination: the model handles the easy cases, the expert stays in control of the rest

Recommender Pipeline

  • MiniRocket features + 16 topography weights → keep-probability → tiers
  • 480 components / 30 participants · ~75% test accuracy
why MiniRocket → see experiments slide; ICLabel question → answer verbally; do NOT volunteer "second-best model".

Recommender Training

def train_recommender(labelled_components):
    X, y = [], []
    for c in labelled_components:        # ICs from 1+ experiments
        ts   = pad_or_truncate(c.time_series)   # uniform length
        feat = MiniRocket().transform(ts)       # 9,996 features
        topo = c.unmixing_row                   # 16 spatial weights
        X.append(concat(feat, topo))            # 10,012-dim vector
        y.append(c.label)                       # keep / reject

    model = RandomForest(n_estimators=100)
    model.fit(X, y)
    save(model)
    return model
backup if asked how the model is trained
labelled components from one or more experiments
each ones time series goes through minirocket for the 9996 features, plus 16 topography weights from the unmixing matrix row
concatenate both, train a random forest with 100 trees
at inference the forests vote share is the keep-probability that drives the tiers

# Part A · Section-by-section summaries

## Front matter
- **Abstract** — Presents ICARUS, a visual analytics system for ICA component keep/reject classification built through a design study with EEG experts, and reports a qualitative two-expert evaluation showing the value comes from combining the building blocks.
- **Acknowledgements** — Thanks supervisors and consultants and notes the PRESENT / HEREDITARY funding.

## 1 · Introduction
- **§ Motivation** — EEG is a core brain-measurement tool but is contaminated by artefacts, and the manual expert review needed to clean it is a slow, subjective bottleneck that limits study scale.
- **§ Problem Statement** — ICA decomposes EEG into components an expert must classify keep/reject, a process that is time-consuming, cognitively demanding, inconsistent, and whose reasoning is never recorded.
- **§ Proposed Solution** — ICARUS is a human-in-the-loop visual analytics system pairing ML keep/reject recommendations across three confidence tiers with coordinated interactive visualisations.
- **§ Research Goal and Contributions** — States RQ1 (which visualisation/interaction techniques support the decision) and RQ2 (how to integrate ML while preserving control), and lists four contributions: domain characterization, the system, the evaluation, and human-in-the-loop evidence.

## 2 · Background
- **§ Electroencephalography** — EEG non-invasively measures scalp voltage from neuronal post-synaptic potentials at millisecond resolution on a standardised 10–10 layout and needs heavy preprocessing.
  - **§§ Frequency Bands** — Defines the five EEG bands (delta, theta, alpha, beta, gamma) and the cognitive states each is associated with.
- **§ Common artefacts in EEG** — Any recorded potential not originating from brain activity is an artefact, classified into physiological and technical categories.
  - **§§ Physiological Artefacts** — Artefacts generated by the participant's own body, mainly the eyes, muscles, and heart.
    - **¶ Ocular Artefacts and Electrooculography** — Eye movements and blinks produce large, slow, low-frequency, frontal signals measured via EOG and commonly removed through ICA/regression.
    - **¶ Cardiac Artefacts and Electrocardiography** — The heartbeat creates rhythmic spike artefacts (more pronounced over the left temporal region) recorded with ECG and removed by deleting the ECG component in ICA.
    - **¶ Muscle Artefacts and Electromyography** — Muscle contractions produce broadband (≈1–200 Hz) artefacts recorded across all sensors that are the hardest to remove without harming the EEG.
  - **§§ Technical Artefacts** — External sources such as 50 Hz power-line interference, electrode/channel noise, movement artefacts, and environmental interference.
- **§ EEG preprocessing** — Raw EEG must be filtered and resampled before ICA, using the pipeline of the consulted domain experts.
  - **¶ Notch Filter** — Removes power-line interference at 50 Hz while leaving neighbouring frequencies largely unaffected.
  - **¶ Band-pass Filter** — A 4th-order elliptic IIR filter isolates the 1–75 Hz pass-band of interest.
  - **¶ Resampling** — Downsamples the data (to 250 Hz) via high-quality polyphase filtering to reduce its size.
  - **¶ EOG Regression** — Uses the EOG channels to remove ocular artefacts from the EEG via linear regression.
- **§ Independent Component Analysis** — ICA is blind source separation (X = A·S, Ŝ = W·X, the cocktail-party problem) that unmixes EEG into independent components, each with a topography, spectrum, and time series, which experts classify keep/reject.
  - **§§ Back-Projection** — The cleaned signal is reconstructed from kept components only, and because this is irreversible a wrong reject deletes real brain signal while a wrong keep leaves noise.
- **§ Visual Analytics** — Defines visual analytics as combining automated analysis with interactive visual interfaces so the analyst's judgement complements the algorithm, especially in human-in-the-loop settings.
- **§ Design Study Methodology** — Introduces Sedlmair et al.'s nine-stage, three-phase, problem-driven methodology for building visualisation tools together with domain experts.
  - **§§ Precondition Phase** — The first three stages prepare the researcher and gather a foundation for the collaboration.
    - **¶ 1. Learn** — Acquire a solid command of the existing visualisation literature, since it informs every later stage.
    - **¶ 2. Winnow** — Identify and carefully select the most promising collaborations, filtering by data availability and a genuine need.
    - **¶ 3. Cast** — Identify the key roles of everyone involved, especially the front-line analyst and the gatekeeper.
    - **¶ 4. Discover** — Characterise and abstract the problem by learning the experts' practices and translating them into tasks and data models.
    - **¶ 5. Design** — Generate and validate data abstractions, visual encodings, and interactions, exploring many options before narrowing to a few.
    - **¶ 6. Implement** — Build software prototypes and the final tool, favouring rapid prototyping for fast feedback.
    - **¶ 7. Deploy** — Release the tool to the experts for real use and gather feedback on its effectiveness.
  - **§§ Analysis Phase** — The final two stages reflect on the project and communicate its results.
    - **¶ 8. Reflect** — Critically analyse the process and outcome to confirm, refine, reject, or propose visualisation design guidelines.
    - **¶ 9. Write** — Write the design study paper, clearly explaining the contributions and lessons learned.

*(Note: the heading source labels the Discover–Deploy block as the "Core Phase"; in the `.tex` the Core-Phase subsection heading sits with stage 4.)*

## 3 · Related Work
- **§ Design Studies in Other Domains** — Reviews design studies such as IRVINE (BMW engine faults) and KAVAGait (clinical gait) that follow the same nine-stage methodology and externalise implicit expert knowledge.
- **§ ICA Classification Workflows** — Surveys the major EEG platforms and automated classifiers (with a capability table) and concludes none combines coordinated detail views, a cross-participant overview, and ML assistance.
  - **§§ Script-Based and GUI-Based Approaches** — MNE-Python, FieldTrip, and EEGLAB share the same ICA primitives but differ in interactivity, lacking linked updates, calibrated confidence, batch-apply, and (mostly) cross-recording views.
    - **¶ MNE-Python** — An open-source Python library with the most complete primitives and a dedicated per-component properties view, but per-artefact detectors only and no unified classifier or calibrated confidence.
    - **¶ FieldTrip** — A MATLAB toolbox where ICA classification is a script-driven manual task with no automated step and no documented cross-recording view.
    - **¶ EEGLAB** — Exposes ICA classification through a GUI with a topographic grid and per-component popups, with cross-subject clustering needing manual setup and the classifier shipping as the bundled ICLabel plugin.
  - **§§ Automated ICA Component Classifiers** — ICLabel (CNN, 7-class), MARA (linear, six features, posterior probability), and ADJUST (threshold rules) are one-shot classifiers with no interactive interface and fixed, incompatible outputs.
- **§ Visualisation Research** — Reviews visualisation work relevant to the system in three strands.
  - **¶ EEG Visualisation** — Reviews EEG-domain projects (Anderson's linked electrode views, Schirrmeister's CNN-decoding visualisations, Knoll's preference-vs-performance prototypes, Slayback's CLIVE/VR topomap) relevant to coordinated and topographic views.
  - **¶ Time Series Visualisation** — Reviews coordinated-multiple-view and brushing techniques for time-oriented data (Aigner's survey, Rakuschek's AnoScout) reused in the system.
  - **¶ Dimensionality Reduction** — Reviews projection methods (PCA, MDS, t-SNE, UMAP) and Fujiwara's MulTiDR, motivating the single fixed UMAP projection of MiniRocket descriptors.
- **§ Human-in-the-Loop Visual Analytics** — Reviews HITL anomaly systems (MTV, HILAD, MANDALA, AnoScout, AnoViz) whose colour-coded tags, cluster-drilldown, and feedback loops informed the design.

## 4 · System Design
- **§ Requirements** — Six requirements derived from discovery interviews, grouped into three categories.
  - **¶ Overview and guidance** — R1 (visual keep/reject recommendations) and R2 (overview of all components for exploration and context).
  - **¶ Component inspection** — R3 (when artefacts appear over time), R4 (distinguish brain from artefact), and R5 (distribution across the scalp).
  - **¶ Workflow** — R6 (log and export classification decisions for reproducibility and team consistency).
- **§ Overall Classification Workflow** — Describes the end-to-end flow: create a study, preprocess, batch-apply high-confidence recommendations, inspect uncertain components, control clusters, export, and optionally retrain.
- **§ Visual Analytics Design** — Describes the visual components and how they satisfy R1–R6.
  - **§§ Recommender Bar** — Maps the ML keep-probability to three confidence tiers (shown as five colour regions) plus a separate MNE muscle badge, with per-participant apply and reset actions.
  - **§§ Component Cluster and Mapper Graph** — Two complementary views of every component across participants in one shared 2D space.
    - **§§§ Component Cluster** — A UMAP scatter where each dot is a component coloured by label (or by confidence), supporting hover, selection, and outlier spotting.
    - **§§§ Mapper Graph** — A DBSCAN-based topological summary grouping nearby components into pie-chart nodes connected by proximity edges.
    - **§§§ Interaction Between Views** — Clicking a Mapper node filters the Cluster, following overview-first, zoom-and-filter, details-on-demand.
  - **§§ Power Spectral Density** — Shows the selected component's frequency spectrum with the five EEG bands highlighted, updating to a selected timeline range.
  - **§§ Topomap** — Shows each scalp sensor's contribution and polarity on a 10–10 head outline, interpolated and normalised across components.
  - **§§ Timeline** — Shows the component's signal over the full recording with zoom/pan and a time-range selection that updates the topomap and PSD.
  - **§§ Component Info and Settings** — Shows component metadata, classification buttons, keyboard navigation, and timeline marker toggles.
    - **¶ EOG Markers** — Orange bars mark detected eye-blink events using MNE blink detection on the Fp1 channel.
    - **¶ Trial Markers** — Light-blue bands shade experimental trial periods read from the XDF marker stream.
    - **¶ PSD Clusters** — K-means on windowed power spectra colour-codes timeline segments by spectral profile, with configurable k/window/step.
- **§ System Architecture** — Summarises the architecture and the pipelines producing the displayed data.
  - **§§ Technology Stack** — Two Docker containers: a Flask backend (MNE, scikit-learn, aeon/MiniRocket, umap-learn) and a React/canvas frontend over REST.
  - **§§ Data Processing Pipeline** — Per-participant preprocessing (notch, elliptic IIR band-pass, resample, FastICA) plus Welch PSD, muscle detection, and EOG/trial event detection.
  - **§§ Machine Learning Pipeline** — Trains a 100-tree Random Forest on concatenated MiniRocket (9,996) and 16 unmixing-matrix-row features, outputting a keep-probability mapped to the three tiers.
  - **§§ Embedding Pipeline** — Extracts MiniRocket features, optionally selects the most informative by label, and projects them with a cosine-distance UMAP.
  - **§§ Cluster Mapping Pipeline** — Builds the Mapper Graph from the UMAP coordinates via DBSCAN nodes and proximity-threshold edges, recomputable after relabelling.
  - **§§ Export and Integration** — Stores each component's keep/reject/undecided state as YAML re-importable into MNE (`ica.exclude` + `ica.apply()`) with preserved preprocessing metadata.

## 5 · Evaluation
- **§ Recommender Experiments** — Documents the systematic experiments on 480 labelled components that produced the final recommender pipeline.
  - **§§ Classification Pipeline** — Nine experiments using participant-level splits and 5-fold CV settled on MiniRocket + topography + Random Forest (~75% test) for speed, even though Matrix Profile reached 79%.
  - **§§ Matrix Profile Experiments** — Matrix-Profile statistics with topography gave the best 79% test accuracy but were dropped because of the expensive self-join at inference.
- **§ Expert Evaluation** — Presents the qualitative deploy-stage evaluation with domain experts on their own data.
  - **§§ Study Design** — A semi-structured interview combined with a think-aloud protocol, framed by four evaluation goals.
  - **§§ Participants** — Two Institute of Neural Engineering experts: E1 (Leitner, interviewed) and E2 (Wriessnegger, indirect).
  - **§§ Procedure** — A single ~45-minute think-aloud session with E1 on two pre-loaded studies.
    - **§§§ Setup** — One unlabelled and one pre-labelled study, both preprocessed with recommendations and embeddings ready.
    - **§§§ Task** — Five tasks covering recommendations, artefact identification, band-power analysis, mapper exploration, and view comparison.
    - **§§§ Post-Task Interview** — Open-ended questions on workflow comparison, useful views, tier usage, disagreements, missing building blocks, and adoption.
  - **§§ Results** — Reports the expert's classification process and per-building-block findings.
    - **§§§ Expert Classification Process** — E1's consistent sequence: component index, topomap, PSD, timeline, then decision (~5 s for clear cases).
    - **§§§ Findings by System Building Block** — Summarises which building blocks worked and which did not.
      - **¶ Recommendations** — The recommender was trusted and strong on ocular artefacts but distrusted on muscle (checked manually), and Tier 1 was trusted in principle but preferred as an indicator rather than pre-set.
      - **¶ Component Overview (UMAP)** — Eye components clustered well and the view was most useful as a global sanity check for outlier detection, catching a misclassified component during the session.
      - **¶ Mapper Graph** — Did not work as a standalone view because its node-and-edge structure mapped to nothing physiologically meaningful.
      - **¶ Timeline** — Played a secondary confirmatory role, with the trial-marker overlay rated its most useful part.
      - **¶ Power Spectral Density** — E1 preferred a continuous PSD curve over the original band-power bars, prompting a redesign.
      - **¶ Topographic Map** — The most valued view for E1 (frontal/peripheral/dipolar patterns), though E2 found it less intuitive.
      - **¶ Labels and Export** — Confusing terminology at first, but once understood was rated a strongest building block for feeding labels back into MNE and retraining the recommender.
  - **§§ Design Revision** — Post-session changes: band-power bars → continuous PSD curve, Mapper demoted to navigation, topomap enlarged, recommendations no longer pre-set, and arrow-key navigation added.
  - **§§ Comparison with MNE-Python Workflow** — Contrasts ICARUS's linked views, template-free cross-participant projection, calibrated tiered recommender, and YAML export against the equivalent MNE script-based workflow.
  - **§§ Summary of Findings** — The layout matched how experts already reason, the recommender helped on ocular but not muscle, and the improvement came from the combination of building blocks rather than any single one.
    - **§§§ Validity of the Evaluation** — Real data, the real task, think-aloud capture, and the iterative refinement loop support the evaluation's validity.
    - **§§§ Limitations of the Evaluation Method** — Only two same-group experts, a single session, no quantitative comparison, and informal post-revision validation.

## 6 · Discussion
- **§ RQ1: Visualisation and Interaction Techniques** — The topomap and PSD are the two primary decision views, the timeline is confirmatory, and the trial markers proved more useful than expected.
- **§ RQ2: Machine Learning Integration with Expert Control** — The three-tier confidence system accelerated clear (ocular) cases while the expert kept control, with recommendations shown as indicators and exported labels enabling retraining.
- **§ Reflections on the Design Study** — The expert interview corrected choices (the Mapper Graph, the band-power chart) that requirements alone missed, and the think-aloud confirmed the chosen view priorities.
- **§ Requirements Revisited** — All six requirements were validated, with R1 and R2 revised after the evaluation session.
- **§ Limitations** — Training data from one study/expert, ~75% accuracy, untested generalisation, no per-decision explanation, and a red/green scheme that is not colour-blind safe.
- **§ Future Work** — Improve muscle classification (e.g. MNE `find_bads_muscle`), gather multi-lab labels, run a controlled quantitative study, add explainability, add undo and collaborative labelling, and project artefact events onto the trial timeline.

## 7 · Conclusion
- **Conclusion** — The system works because its building blocks work together rather than from any single novelty, and the design-study process — especially the think-aloud protocol — both shaped and corrected the tool.
